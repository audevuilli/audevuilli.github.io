The Guardian Recipes

This project  explores over 7,000 food recipes published in The Guardian newspaper from 2009 to 2019. I published a blog post serie to discuss the results of the exploratory data analysis (part I) and the topic modelling (part II). I employed web scraping, text mining and Natural Language Processing (NLP) to provide insight into the rise and decline of food ingredients, culinary trends and influential chefs.

Part I

What are the chefs having contributed the most to the culinary collection of The Guardian? Which recipes have been the most successful? What are the dishes that have become popular in the last past years?

If you are interested to find answers to these question, please, have a read at the first part of my "Guardian Recipes" analysis.

Part II

What are the main topics of The Guardian recipes? Is it possible to categorize the chefs by their cuisine, to classify their recipes by their ingredients?

This second blogpost uses LDA Topic Modelling to classify each recipe and find out the cuisine of each chef. To learn more about it, please, have a read at the second part of my "Guardian Recipes"!

Food Culture and Tradition

Food plays an important role in our customs. Seasons, feasts, celebrations and daily routines are accompanied by meals that have more or less significance. I used machine learning techniques (text mining, natural language processing) to investigate diverse food trends and traditions.

Pancake Day 2019

Pancake Day or Shrove Tuesday has its tradition in Christianism. This day is celebrated by eating pancakes and thus, finishing all the butter, eggs and fat that are to be avoided during the Lenten season (the 40 days before Easter). Nowadays, Pancake Day is a day of pleasure, joy, and baking, and gives a perfect occasion for everyone to get active on social media. Find more about the content of 70,000 tweets by reading my blogpost.

Oxford Farming Conference

Each January, the well-established Oxford Farming Conference (OFC) and the youngest Oxford Real Farming Conference (ORFC) took place in Oxford. Farmers, politicians and scientists meet to discuss the future of the british agriculture and food sector.

This blogpost wrap-up the debates that happened during both conferences by analysing more than 3,500 tweets. I used Natural Language Processing, Sentiment Analysis, and Topic Modelling. Find more about the actors of 2020 UK agricultural system and  about their answers to tackle today's agriculture challenges by reading my analysis.
